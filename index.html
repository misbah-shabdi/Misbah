TUBERCULOSIS

Bacterial infection that usually affects the lungs but can spread to other parts of the body.


"Fight TB before it attacks you"

Overview

  • “Every year, 10 million people fall ill with tuberculosis (TB)”. Despite being a preventable and curable disease, 1.5 million people die from TB each year – making it the world’s top infectious killer.
  • TB is the leading cause of death of people with HIV and also a major contributor to antimicrobial resistance. Most of the people who fall ill with TB live in low- and middle-income countries, but TB is present all over the world. About half of all people with TB can be found in 8 countries: [Bangladesh, China, India, Indonesia, Nigeria, Pakistan, Philippines and South Africa].
  • About a quarter of the global population is estimated to have been infected with TB bacteria, but most people will not go on to develop TB disease and some will clear the infection. Those who are infected but not (yet) ill with the disease cannot transmit it.
  • People infected with TB bacteria have a 5–10% lifetime risk of falling ill with TB. Those with compromised immune systems, such as people living with HIV, malnutrition or diabetes, or people who use tobacco, have a higher risk of falling ill.

About TB

  • Tuberculosis (TB) is caused by a bacterium called Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
  • TB usually affects the lungs, but can also affect the brain, kidneys, spine, and more.
  • TB can affect multiple parts of the body at the same time.
  • Two TB-related conditions exist: inactive TB and active TB disease.
  • Getting tested and treated for TB protects yourself and your community.

Prevention and Diagnosis

Overview

TB is preventable and curable. If you are at risk for TB, talking with your healthcare provider is an essential first step to protecting your family, friends, and community from this highly contagious disease.

TB Treatment

  • TB is typically treated with a standard six-month course of four antibiotics. Common drugs include rifampicin and isoniazid.
  • In some cases, TB bacteria do not respond to standard drugs, leading to drug-resistant TB. Treatment for drug-resistant TB is longer and more complex.

Prevention Measures for TB Patients

  1. Stay Home: Do not go to work or school.
  2. Isolate at Home: Spend minimal time with household members and sleep in a separate room.
  3. Ventilate the Room: Tuberculosis germs spread more easily in closed spaces. If possible, open windows and use fans to circulate air.
  4. Wear a Face Mask: When around others, wear a mask and encourage household members to wear masks for added protection.
  5. Cover Your Mouth: Use a tissue to cover your mouth when sneezing or coughing, seal it in a bag, and dispose of it properly.

Vaccination

In countries where TB is common, infants are often vaccinated with the bacille Calmette-Guerin (BCG) vaccine, which protects infants and toddlers, especially from TB disease in the fluid surrounding the brain and spinal cord.

Testing for Tuberculosis

  1. Types of Tests:
    • TB Blood Test
    • TB Skin Test
  2. Positive Test Result: Indicates the presence of TB germs in the body.
  3. Further Testing: If you test positive, your healthcare provider may conduct additional tests to determine if you have inactive TB (latent TB infection) or active TB disease.
  4. TB Skin Test: Can also help identify TB germs in the body.

Diagnosis Process

Initial Evaluation

Anyone with TB symptoms or a positive TB test result should be evaluated by a healthcare provider for TB disease.

Medical Evaluation for TB Disease

  • Diagnosis: Your healthcare provider may diagnose inactive TB or active TB disease based on:
    1. Medical history
    2. Physical examination
    3. TB infection tests (blood test or skin test)
    4. Chest x-ray
    5. Laboratory tests (sputum smear and culture) to detect TB germs
    6. Laboratory tests for drug resistance

Medical History

  • Questions You May Be Asked:
    1. Do you have symptoms of TB disease?
    2. Have you spent time with someone with active TB?
    3. Have you ever had a positive TB blood test or TB skin test?
    4. Have you previously been diagnosed with inactive TB or TB disease?
    5. Do you have factors that may increase your risk for TB?

Physical Examination

  • Purpose of Physical Examination:
    1. Provides insights into overall health
    2. Assists in the diagnosis
    3. Identifies other factors that may influence TB treatment
